Stress

It is difficult to give a short answer to the question "what is stress?". Here, we will explain it via the more understandable traction vector. Then, via Cauchy's theorem, we can introduce the notion Cauchy stress tensor σ\boldsymbol{ \sigma}σ. With the definition of σ\boldsymbol{ \sigma}σ, we can derive the equilibrium equation for a continuum body. Finally, we will show that equilibrium also requires that σ\boldsymbol{ \sigma}σ is a symmetric tensor.

The traction vector

Consider that we have a loaded structure (left), and then we make a plane cut through this structure (green line). In order to replace the internal forces acting on the cut plane, we need to introduce forces. These can be both perpendicular to the cut plane and along the cut plane. In reality, the forces are discrete between atoms in the material. However, in continuum mechanics, we introduce this as a continuous field of force per area, which we define as traction. More precisely, we consider an infitesimal area dA\mathrm{d} AdA of this cut surface with normal vector n‾\underline{\boldsymbol{ n}}n​ (right). The sum of force vectors acting on this surface is dF‾\underline{\boldsymbol{ \mathrm{d} F}}dF​ and the traction is then defined as

t‾:=dF‾dA\begin{aligned} \underline{\boldsymbol{ t}} := \frac{\underline{\boldsymbol{ \mathrm{d} F}}}{\mathrm{d} A} \end{aligned}t​:=dAdF​​​

Cauchy's theorem

Above, we defined the traction vector t‾\underline{\boldsymbol{ t}}t​. For a loaded body, the traction vector, t‾\underline{\boldsymbol{ t}}t​, depends on both the position in the body and the normal vector of the cut plane. It would be convenient to have a measure of the internal forces (per area) in the body that doesn't depend on the exact cut plane. To solve this, we consider the tetrahedron below.

Relationship between areas

To be able to use this, we need to determine the relationship between the four areas. To this end, we will use the divergence theorem. If we consider a vector function a‾(x‾)\underline{\boldsymbol{ a}}(\underline{\boldsymbol{ x}})a​(x​) defined inside and in the viscinity of a closed body Ω\OmegaΩ with the boundary Γ\GammaΓ, the Gauss (divergence) theorem states

∫Ωa‾⋅∇‾dΩ=∫Γa‾⋅n‾dΓ∫Ωaj∇jdΩ=∫ΓajnjdΓ\begin{aligned} \int_\Omega \underline{\boldsymbol{ a}}\cdot\underline{\boldsymbol{ \nabla}} \mathrm{d} \Omega &= \int_\Gamma \underline{\boldsymbol{ a}}\cdot\underline{\boldsymbol{ n}} \mathrm{d} \Gamma \\ \int_\Omega a_j \nabla_j \mathrm{d} \Omega &= \int_\Gamma a_j n_j \mathrm{d} \Gamma \end{aligned}∫Ω​a​⋅∇​dΩ∫Ω​aj​∇j​dΩ​=∫Γ​a​⋅n​dΓ=∫Γ​aj​nj​dΓ​

where n‾\underline{\boldsymbol{ n}}n​ is a unit normal vector pointing out of the body. By using this equation three times with b1j=ajb_{1j}=a_jb1j​=aj​, b2j=ajb_{2j}=a_jb2j​=aj​, and lastly b3j=ajb_{3j}=a_jb3j​=aj​ we get three equations as

∫Ωbij∇jdΩ=∫ΓbijnjdΓ\begin{aligned} \int_\Omega b_{ij} \nabla_j \mathrm{d} \Omega &= \int_\Gamma b_{ij} n_j \mathrm{d} \Gamma \end{aligned}∫Ω​bij​∇j​dΩ​=∫Γ​bij​nj​dΓ​

If we then set bij=δijb_{ij}=\delta_{ij}bij​=δij​, we obtain

∫ΓnidΓ=0\begin{aligned} \int_\Gamma n_i \mathrm{d} \Gamma = 0 \end{aligned}∫Γ​ni​dΓ=0​

as δij∇j\delta_{ij} \nabla_jδij​∇j​ is zero as δij\delta_{ij}δij​ is constant. For our tetrahedron, this implies that

An‾=A1e‾1+A2e‾2+A3e‾3=Aie‾i\begin{aligned} A \underline{\boldsymbol{ n}} = A_1 \underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ 1} + A_2 \underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ 2} + A_3 \underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ 3} = A_i \underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ i} \end{aligned}An​=A1​e​1​+A2​e​2​+A3​e​3​=Ai​e​i​​

where n‾\underline{\boldsymbol{ n}}n​ is the normal vector of the plane with area AAA. The three other faces have normal vectors n‾1=−e‾1\underline{\boldsymbol{ n}}_1=-\underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ 1}n​1​=−e​1​, n‾2=−e‾2\underline{\boldsymbol{ n}}_2=-\underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ 2}n​2​=−e​2​, and n‾3=−e‾3\underline{\boldsymbol{ n}}_3=-\underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ 3}n​3​=−e​3​. Scalar multiplying this expression by e‾j\underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ j}e​j​ we obtain

An‾⋅e‾j=Aiδij=Aj\begin{aligned} A \underline{\boldsymbol{ n}}\cdot\underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ j} = A_i \delta_{ij} = A_j \end{aligned}An​⋅e​j​=Ai​δij​=Aj​​

Equilibrium on tetrahedron

We consider that the tetrahedron can include a body load which is load per volume, b‾\underline{\boldsymbol{ b}}b​, where the volume is VVV. Then, the equilibrium equation for the tetrahedron becomes

0=t‾iAi+t‾A+b‾V\begin{aligned} 0 = \underline{\boldsymbol{ t}}_i A_i + \underline{\boldsymbol{ t}} A + \underline{\boldsymbol{ b}} V \end{aligned}0=t​i​Ai​+t​A+b​V​

Evaluating this equation for each coordinate direction, e‾j\underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ j}e​j​, we get

0=Ait‾i⋅e‾j+At‾⋅e‾j+Vb‾⋅e‾j\begin{aligned} 0 &= A_i \underline{\boldsymbol{ t}}_i \cdot \underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ j} + A \underline{\boldsymbol{ t}} \cdot \underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ j} + V \underline{\boldsymbol{ b}} \cdot \underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ j} \end{aligned}0​=Ai​t​i​⋅e​j​+At​⋅e​j​+Vb​⋅e​j​​

Inserting Equation (5), we obtain

0=A[n‾⋅e‾i][t‾i⋅e‾j]+At‾⋅e‾j+Vb‾⋅e‾j0=[n‾⋅e‾i][t‾i⋅e‾j]+t‾⋅e‾j+VAb‾⋅e‾j\begin{aligned} 0 &= A [\underline{\boldsymbol{ n}}\cdot\underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ i}] [\underline{\boldsymbol{ t}}_i \cdot \underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ j}] + A \underline{\boldsymbol{ t}} \cdot \underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ j} + V \underline{\boldsymbol{ b}} \cdot \underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ j} \\ 0 &= [\underline{\boldsymbol{ n}}\cdot\underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ i}] [\underline{\boldsymbol{ t}}_i \cdot \underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ j}] + \underline{\boldsymbol{ t}} \cdot \underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ j} + \frac{V}{A} \underline{\boldsymbol{ b}} \cdot \underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ j} \end{aligned}00​=A[n​⋅e​i​][t​i​⋅e​j​]+At​⋅e​j​+Vb​⋅e​j​=[n​⋅e​i​][t​i​⋅e​j​]+t​⋅e​j​+AV​b​⋅e​j​​

If we now let the tetrahedron shrink, the volume to area ratio goes to zero (because volume is proportional to the side lengths cubed and area to lengths squared). Hence, we get

[n‾⋅e‾i][t‾i⋅e‾j]+t‾⋅e‾j=0\begin{aligned} [\underline{\boldsymbol{ n}}\cdot\underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ i}] [\underline{\boldsymbol{ t}}_i \cdot \underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ j}] + \underline{\boldsymbol{ t}} \cdot \underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ j} = 0 \end{aligned}[n​⋅e​i​][t​i​⋅e​j​]+t​⋅e​j​=0​

Let us denote the quantity −t‾i⋅e‾j=σij-\underline{\boldsymbol{ t}}_i \cdot \underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ j}=\sigma_{ij}−t​i​⋅e​j​=σij​. Noting that ni=n‾⋅e‾in_i=\underline{\boldsymbol{ n}}\cdot\underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ i}ni​=n​⋅e​i​ is the component of n‾\underline{\boldsymbol{ n}}n​ in the e‾i\underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ i}e​i​ direction, and tj=t‾⋅e‾jt_j=\underline{\boldsymbol{ t}} \cdot \underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ j}tj​=t​⋅e​j​ is the component of t‾\underline{\boldsymbol{ t}}t​ in the e‾j\underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_{ j}e​j​ direction, we have

niσij=tj\begin{aligned} n_i \sigma_{ij} = t_j \end{aligned}ni​σij​=tj​​

Or, in tensor form

n‾⋅σ=t‾\begin{aligned} \underline{\boldsymbol{ n}}\cdot \boldsymbol{ \sigma} = \underline{\boldsymbol{ t}} \end{aligned}n​⋅σ=t​​

Equation (6) is Cauchy's Theorem. We see that the 2nd order tensor σ\boldsymbol{ \sigma}σ can describe the traction on plane at a point in the body. We have thus achieved our goal that σ\boldsymbol{ \sigma}σ now describes the load in the body independent of which direction we cut the body in. With this information, let's use that to formulate the equilibrium equations!

Equilibrium

In the previous example, we took the equilibrium to find the definition of the Cauchy stress. In the process, we saw that the the area terms where dominating over the volume terms as we went to an infitesimal volume. But let's do it again, now considering that the stress may vary over our small volume. For simplicity, we consider a regular hexahedron.

The traction t‾1\underline{\boldsymbol{ t}}_1t​1​ will change slightly as we move from x0x_0x0​ to x1=x0+dxx_1=x_0+\mathrm{d} xx1​=x0​+dx, but we consider it's average value in the yyy and zzz directions. Similarly for t‾2\underline{\boldsymbol{ t}}_2t​2​ and t‾3\underline{\boldsymbol{ t}}_3t​3​ for x,zx,zx,z and x,yx,yx,y respectively. Taking the equilibrium equations for a volume load b‾\underline{\boldsymbol{ b}}b​ and neglecting dynamic forces (i.e. quasi-static conditions) in the direction e‾i\underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_ie​i​ we obtain

[[(t‾1(x0)+t‾1(x1)]dydz+[(t‾2(y0)+t‾2(y1)]dxdz+[(t‾3(z0)+t‾1(z1)]dxdy]⋅e‾i=−b‾dV\begin{aligned} \left[\left[(\underline{\boldsymbol{ t}}_1(x_0)+\underline{\boldsymbol{ t}}_1(x_1)\right] \mathrm{d} y \mathrm{d} z + \left[(\underline{\boldsymbol{ t}}_2(y_0)+\underline{\boldsymbol{ t}}_2(y_1)\right] \mathrm{d} x \mathrm{d} z + \left[(\underline{\boldsymbol{ t}}_3(z_0)+\underline{\boldsymbol{ t}}_1(z_1)\right] \mathrm{d} x \mathrm{d} y\right] \cdot \underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_i = -\underline{\boldsymbol{ b}} \mathrm{d} V \end{aligned}[[(t​1​(x0​)+t​1​(x1​)]dydz+[(t​2​(y0​)+t​2​(y1​)]dxdz+[(t​3​(z0​)+t​1​(z1​)]dxdy]⋅e​i​=−b​dV​

Inserting Cauchy's Theorem, and noting that the normal vectors are ±e‾j\pm \underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_j±e​j​, we get

[[σ(x1)−σ(x0)]⋅e‾1dydz+[σ(y1)−σ(y0)]⋅e‾2dxdz+[σ(z1)−σ(z0)]⋅e‾3dxdy]⋅e‾i=−b‾e‾idV\begin{aligned} \left[\left[\boldsymbol{ \sigma}(x_1)-\boldsymbol{ \sigma}(x_0)\right]\cdot \underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_1 \mathrm{d} y \mathrm{d} z + \left[\boldsymbol{ \sigma}(y_1)-\boldsymbol{ \sigma}(y_0)\right]\cdot \underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_2 \mathrm{d} x \mathrm{d} z + \left[\boldsymbol{ \sigma}(z_1)-\boldsymbol{ \sigma}(z_0)\right]\cdot \underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_3 \mathrm{d} x \mathrm{d} y\right] \cdot \underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_i = -\underline{\boldsymbol{ b}} \underline{\boldsymbol{ e}}_i \mathrm{d} V \end{aligned}[[σ(x1​)−σ(x0​)]⋅e​1​dydz+[σ(y1​)−σ(y0​)]⋅e​2​dxdz+[σ(z1​)−σ(z0​)]⋅e​3​dxdy]⋅e​i​=−b​e​i​dV​

where dV=dxdydz\mathrm{d} V = \mathrm{d} x \mathrm{d} y \mathrm{d} zdV=dxdydz. Changing to full index notation we get

[σi1(x1)−σi1(x0)]dydz+[σi2(y1)−σi2(y0)]dxdz+[σi3(z1)−σi3(z0)]dxdy=−bidV\begin{aligned} \left[\sigma_{i1}(x_1)-\sigma_{i1}(x_0)\right] \mathrm{d} y \mathrm{d} z + \left[\sigma_{i2}(y_1)-\sigma_{i2}(y_0)\right] \mathrm{d} x \mathrm{d} z + \left[\sigma_{i3}(z_1)-\sigma_{i3}(z_0)\right] \mathrm{d} x \mathrm{d} y = -b_i \mathrm{d} V \end{aligned}[σi1​(x1​)−σi1​(x0​)]dydz+[σi2​(y1​)−σi2​(y0​)]dxdz+[σi3​(z1​)−σi3​(z0​)]dxdy=−bi​dV​

Dividing by dV=dxdydz\mathrm{d} V=\mathrm{d} x \mathrm{d} y \mathrm{d} zdV=dxdydz, we obtain

σi1(x0+dx)−σi1(x0)dx+σi2(y0+dy)−σi2(y0)dy+σi3(z0+dz)−σi3(z0)dz=−bi\begin{aligned} \frac{\sigma_{i1}(x_0+\mathrm{d} x)-\sigma_{i1}(x_0)}{\mathrm{d} x} + \frac{\sigma_{i2}(y_0+\mathrm{d} y)-\sigma_{i2}(y_0)}{\mathrm{d} y} + \frac{\sigma_{i3}(z_0+\mathrm{d} z)-\sigma_{i3}(z_0)}{\mathrm{d} z} = -b_i \end{aligned}dxσi1​(x0​+dx)−σi1​(x0​)​+dyσi2​(y0​+dy)−σi2​(y0​)​+dzσi3​(z0​+dz)−σi3​(z0​)​=−bi​​

Letting dx→0\mathrm{d} x \rightarrow 0dx→0, dy→0\mathrm{d} y \rightarrow 0dy→0, and dz→0\mathrm{d} z \rightarrow 0dz→0, we obtain

dσi1dx+dσi2dy+dσi3dz=dσijdxj=−bi\begin{aligned} \frac{\mathrm{d} \sigma_{i1}}{\mathrm{d} x} + \frac{\mathrm{d} \sigma_{i2}}{\mathrm{d} y} + \frac{\mathrm{d} \sigma_{i3}}{\mathrm{d} z} = \frac{\mathrm{d} \sigma_{ij}}{\mathrm{d} x_j} = -b_i \end{aligned}dxdσi1​​+dydσi2​​+dzdσi3​​=dxj​dσij​​=−bi​​

which we can identify as the divergence of σ\boldsymbol{ \sigma}σ:

σ⋅∇‾+b‾=0‾\begin{aligned} \boldsymbol{ \sigma}\cdot\underline{\boldsymbol{ \nabla}} + \underline{\boldsymbol{ b}} = \underline{\boldsymbol{ 0}} \end{aligned}σ⋅∇​+b​=0​​

And this is the force equilibrium equation for a continuum.
